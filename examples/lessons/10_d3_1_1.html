You and your students might select independent reading texts from the options provided to build additional knowledge on the topics or texts in the unit. If you have a media specialist or librarian, you can share this list with them to see what resources are available and to generate an extended list of related resources.

This may stem from personal preference or from teacher or school guidance or policy.

If you are using this unit in a remote- or virtual-learning context, you might consult two resources: the general Odell Education Remote Learning Guide and the unit-specific Things Fall Apart Remote Learning Guide. The Odell Education Remote Learning Guide provides general guidance for strategies and structures that can be used for synchronous and asynchronous learning, flipped classrooms, and blended learning. The Things Fall Apart Remote Learning Guide provides remote learning suggestions for each lesson.

STUDENT SUPPORT AND DIFFERENTIATION

This unit, like all units in the Odell Education High School Literacy Program, employs a suite of tools and resources from the Odell Education Literacy Toolbox as well as tools specific to the unit’s content and instructional sequences. Students can be supported throughout the unit by using materials from the Literacy Toolbox, beyond those that are referenced in the unit. Tools are graphic organizers that support students’ reading and writing by helping them think through text, find pertinent textual details, and develop ideas for their writing. Note that many, such as the Analyzing Relationships Tool and the Forming Evidence-Based Claims Tool are text agnostic—they are designed to be used with any text. Others, such as some note-taking tools, have instructions or structures that are specific to the unit topic or text. These tools help students develop and internalize analytical processes. Since they are scaffolds, they can be assigned at your discretion, or students might develop their own system for using them if they encounter difficult sections of text. See the Tools Reference Guide for a list of core tools.

Reference guides provide centralized explanations and examples of concepts and processes common to English language arts. They make important connections to the pertinent tools and provide additional resources related to the concept. The Assessing Sources Reference Guide provides a process to assess sources for bias, credibility, and accuracy, and the Tone Reference Guide provides information for understanding and identifying tone in a text. While activities suggest when to use reference guides during instruction, you might refer students to them throughout the unit to reinforce understanding around a particular concept or process.

Tools and reference guides specific to lessons and activities can be found in their respective Materials tabs. The teaching notes might also suggest tools or reference guides as additional supports for students. Since these supplemental materials are not mentioned in the student directions, they do not appear in the Materials tab. They can be found in the Literacy Toolbox located on the grade level’s homepage under Program Resources.

Chinua Achebe’s (1930-2013) masterful trilogy begins with Things Fall Apart,a novel set in pre-colonial Nigeria and often called “The Great African novel.” Born in an Igbo village in Nigeria, Achebe grew up in colonized Nigeria, where he was influenced by both Christian and Igbo beliefs. Things Fall Apart is followed by two additional novels, No Longer at Ease and Arrow of God, which follow the story. Achebe’s awards include the Man Booker International Prize for fiction and the Dorothy and Lillian Gish Prize.

When referring to the novel in this unit, all pages refer to the following version: Achebe, Chinua. Things Fall Apart. Random House, 1992 [ISBN: 978-0-679-44623-1].

TEACHING STRATEGIES AND DECISIONS

You might choose to expand this activity with an Anticipation Guide. The use of an Anticipation Guide activates students' prior knowledge and builds curiosity about a new topic. Before reading, students respond to several statements that challenge or support their preconceived ideas about key concepts in the text. You should encourage students to draw on their cultural and social backgrounds to make connections. Anticipation Guides challenge students to think critically about what they know—or do not know—about a topic. Anticipation Guides can be revisited later to evaluate how well students understood the material and to correct any misconceptions.

Other strategies to activate background knowledge include using the K-W-L protocol or a Notice and Wonder table. The K-W-L (Know, Want to Know, Learn) protocol asks students to identify what they know about the topic at the beginning of the unit, lesson, or activity; generate questions about what they want to know about the topic; and, at the end, determine what they learned about the topic. In a Notice and Wonder table, students take note about parts of the text in one column and write "I wonder" statements based on those observations in a second column.

STUDENT SUPPORT AND DIFFERENTIATION

The activity approaches student learning through an asset based lens. Students are encouraged to bring the knowledge, insight, and curiosity they already have to enhance their experience in the unit. English learners in particular benefit from making connections to their cultural and social backgrounds.

Students can use the Culminating Task Checklist to determine what knowledge and skills they will need to succeed on the Culminating Task.

Progress monitoring—being aware of one’s own progress and using it to determine what skills and knowledge still need to be mastered—improves student progress. As students become more aware of their own performance, they learn how to more accurately predict their future performance on a range of other tasks. By identifying and self-evaluating initial mastery levels, students can focus on the skills and knowledge they need to build. Finally, this kind of practice can help you make more finely tuned instructional decisions because you have feedback from students about the skills they need to practice.

TEACHING STRATEGIES AND DECISIONS

You might ask students to complete the checklist at the beginning of the unit and after each Section Diagnostic. Consider collecting the checklists as students complete them throughout the unit to monitor how they are evaluating their progress. Such information will help you make instructional decisions that are finely tuned to students’ learning needs.

STUDENT SUPPORT AND DIFFERENTIATION

Some groups might need support in unpacking the Culminating Task prompt and the evaluation criteria. You might check on students individually to probe their understanding of what is being asked of them. Using that information, you might end the activity with a whole-class discussion to ensure the students have a clear understanding of the task.
