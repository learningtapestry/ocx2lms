ABOUT THE AUTHOR, CONCEPT, TEXT, TOPIC

Rather than completing isolated language exercises, students can effectively learn style and conventions by focusing on the writing they are studying. The complex texts used in core instruction provide a rich resource of mentor sentences for students to analyze, and the short written responses, Section Diagnostics, and Culminating Task provide students the opportunity to apply their new learning in an authentic writing context.

Asking students to analyze the structure of a mentor sentence allows them to understand how the parts (e.g., words, phrases, clauses) and structure of the sentence affect their understanding of the text and the writer’s craft. Not all sentences merit a close examination: if the primary goal is understanding a text, sentences that students examine should contain important information or ideas from a text or the essential questions for a unit. The sentence is likely one that has multiple layers of meaning, as it will be read multiple times. If the primary goal is understanding the writer’s craft, sentences should contain words and phrases that relate to the grammatical concepts being explored (e.g., parallelism; types of phrases, such as absolute or participle phrases; clauses; transitions for claims and counterclaims; or conjunctions).

The purpose of the Mentor Sentence Journal is to encourage students to read like writers and to build a writer’s toolbox of writing strategies to use in their own writing. By using the journal, students will have a consistent place to collect their "to use" writing strategies, which can be referenced when writing their responses to the Section Diagnostics and the Culminating Task.

TEACHING STRATEGIES AND DECISIONS

You might choose to use the Working with Mentor Sentences Tool for this activity.

While it may seem counterintuitive to have students turn sentences into passive voice, this will give them a good understanding of how the placement of subject and object changes the impact of a sentence.

Before beginning this exercise, you might have a whole-class discussion about active and passive voice, providing accompanying examples of each.

STUDENT SUPPORT AND DIFFERENTIATION

You might have English learners think about how the construction of a mentor sentence compares to the construction of sentences in their home language, in order to build connections from one language to another.

If students struggle to understand the mentor sentences, you might have them look for examples of the concept in their independent reading, or you might have them manipulate sentence strips so they can clearly see the different parts of a sentence.

If students master the concepts quickly, you might have them experiment with grammatical rules to create sentences that move beyond the model. For example, if students conceptually understand that a semicolon links independent clauses, you might encourage them to use a semicolon to link more than two independent clauses, for effect.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR, CONCEPT, TEXT, TOPIC

The Analyzing Relationships Tool supports and guides a process for identifying and analyzing how an author uses details in a text to create literary effects like mood or tone. You can use this tool to analyze how the author’s choices contribute to effects the text has on you as a reader and the meaning you find in the text. Using this tool usually begins with a guiding or text-specific question related to a literary element or device (e.g., setting, characterization, irony, figurative language). The tool can be used with both literary and informational texts, whenever you are analyzing the relationships among textual details.
