ABOUT THE AUTHOR, CONCEPT, TEXT, TOPIC

“Igbo Culture and History” is the introduction to some versions of Things Fall Apart. Authored by Don Ohadike, PhD (1941-2005), a leading scholar and historian of Igbo history, the essay provides a primer to the Igbo people and their culture. Dr. Ohadike was born in Nigeria, but ended his academic career at Cornell University. Nigeria is a coastal country in West Africa, bordered by Cameroon, Chad, Niger, and Benin. The Igbo people are an ethnic group native to present day Nigeria and Equatorial Guinea.

Things Fall Apart addresses the colonization of Nigeria, a concept likely to be unfamiliar to students. Throughout the unit, students return to this primer to learn more about specific aspects of the Igbo. In this introductory activity, students become familiar with the Igbo and think about a culture that is likely different from their own. Students should approach the text and history with respectful curiosity, especially parts of the culture that might be different or unique.

Annotation is the act of adding notes to a text. Annotating allows students to track their thinking as they are reading. Asking students to annotate the text and take notes as they read can have many pedagogical purposes, such as the following:

  • attending to details about plot, character, and the author’s craft
  • making inferences
  • making connections among different parts of the text, other texts, and students’ own lives
  • asking questions about points of confusion
  • defining unknown words and phrases
  • summarizing or paraphrasing what is happening in the text
  • writing any thoughts or emotions the text elicits

Annotating and note-taking can also help students internalize many of the practices of skilled readers, such as the following:

  • questioning their understanding as they read
  • assimilating new aspects of plot, character, and setting with their prior knowledge and understanding from reading earlier parts of the text

TEACHING STRATEGIES AND DECISIONS

Students might not know where Nigeria is. You can show students Nigeria on a map.

Developing a system for annotating takes practice. You might use the sample annotation key in the Annotating and Note-Taking Reference Guide to model how to annotate the text.

There are different techniques that you might use to help students take notes, such as the following:

  • If students can write on the text, you might ask them to use different marks to indicate different types of annotations (e.g., put an exclamation point next to important points in the text or a question mark next to places where they have questions).
  • You might have students use Post-it notes to write down their annotations and place them on the relevant pages.
  • You might provide students with a two-column chart so they can write the text on the left-hand column and write down their annotation on the right.

The annotations you ask students to write down should connect to your goals for the lesson and unit. Students’ annotations can help you assess their understanding of various aspects of the text.

The following terms should be introduced to students before they read:

  • Harmattan: a dry, dusty easterly or northeasterly wind on the West African coast, occurring from December to February.
  • Breaking the kola: Breaking the kola nut is a ceremony to welcome visitors to their home or village. Kola nut was also originally used to make cola soft drinks and is often chewed to relieve hunger. It is considered sacred in Igbo tradition.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR, CONCEPT, TEXT, TOPIC

Students track their words in a Vocabulary Journal to keep track of words and their definitions throughout the unit. Vocabulary Journals play an important role in building new vocabulary.

Throughout the unit, students also interact with the Vocabulary in Context Tool. The purpose of the Vocabulary in Context Tool is twofold:

  • Students build vocabulary and develop a strategy for determining the meaning of unknown words and phrases when there is contextual information and when there is not.
  • Students metacognate on their process for determining the meaning of unknown words, determine the effectiveness of that process, and articulate a plan for using it in future texts.

You might choose to have the students add to this tool throughout the unit for continued vocabulary support and to develop their abilities in using context clues to discern the meanings of unfamiliar words.

TEACHING STRATEGIES AND DECISIONS

Suggested vocabulary words can be found in the Vocabulary List for each major text in the unit. The Vocabulary List is by no means a comprehensive list—each student will need support with different words. For this reason, students should be encouraged to add unfamiliar or interesting words or concepts to their Vocabulary Journals as they encounter them.

Explain to students that in a text, there will be some words that can be defined using context and some that cannot. If appropriate, you might model how to use the Vocabulary in Context Tool in this activity.

You might assign a certain dictionary, such as Merriam Webster or Oxford English Dictionary, to ensure accuracy. Students should keep individual Vocabulary Journals, as the unit has students add to and use their journals throughout the lessons. You might also have students work collaboratively to create a Word Wall that is maintained and revisited throughout the unit.

STUDENT SUPPORT AND DIFFERENTIATION

When having students define words using contextual clues, keep in mind that the complexity of the context clues might vary greatly depending on the student’s linguistic and cultural background. These differences are grounded in students’ understanding not only of language, but of content as well. For example, native English speakers might grasp nuances in contextual clues, such as tone or cultural references, while English learners might not understand, making it all the more difficult for them to define the targeted vocabulary word.

You might have students draw a picture next to applicable vocabulary and definitions. Mental images and associations can help strengthen students’ understanding of newly acquired vocabulary. The picture does not necessarily need to be a precise depiction; rather, the mental image should serve as an association between the meaning of the word and students’ personal knowledge. For example, for the word contingent, a student may draw an award received for an accomplishment that was contingent on practice and preparation. If students are struggling with envisioning an image for a word, you might have them conduct an image search for the word online and then select an image related to the word’s meaning that resonates with them. Visual supports for key vocabulary are especially beneficial for English learners.

Additionally, if a word under study is a cognate—a word that shares similar spelling, meaning, and pronunciation with a word in another language—in the student’s home language, you might make connections between the cognate and the new vocabulary word. A cognate provides a bridge to the English language for English learners.

STUDENT SUPPORT AND DIFFERENTIATION

Some students might struggle with comprehending the complex text. Consider the following questions to help you provide support:

  • Are students missing requisite background, prior, historical, or cultural knowledge? If so, would they benefit from a short text or video that builds background knowledge?
  • Are students struggling to determine the central and supporting ideas? If so, they might benefit from the following:
  • focused annotation and a short discussion. See the Annotating and Note-Taking Reference Guide.
  • rereading the text multiple times using guiding or text-specific questions, each with a different purpose. See the guiding questions in the Questioning Reference Guide for examples of additional text-dependent questions.
  • using a Reading Closely Tool, such as the Attending to Details Tool
  • walking through a model analysis of a passage from the text
  • additional time to reread and analyze the text
  • Are students struggling with the vocabulary and language? If so, would nonlinguistic representations of concepts be helpful? Preteaching key vocabulary with visual supports can be especially beneficial for English learners.
  • Are students struggling with the length of the sentences or syntax? If so, would they benefit from chunking sentences into easily understandable parts?

Some students who demonstrate advanced competency might benefit from an additional challenge. Consider the following questions, designed to push students:

  • Would students benefit from being asked how this text or topic connects to another text or topic they have read in another unit?
  • Would students benefit from creating analogous relationships?
  • Would students benefit from a task that requires them to discover the symbolic connection between the text and another concept they have learned in this course or elsewhere?
  • Would students benefit from explaining their expertise about the text to a group of novices? (e.g., How would you explain this text to a five-year-old?)
